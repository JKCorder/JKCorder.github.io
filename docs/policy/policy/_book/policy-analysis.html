2.3 Accounting for costs and benefits

Direct (target population)

Direct benefits - intended effects of intervention Unintended consequences (positive or negative) Direct costs - negative Spillover effects Positive: education has a positive spillover to health and citizenship (confined to target population) Could also be negative

Indirect (not target population)

Externalities Negative: development may displace economically vulnerable (particular) Indirect costs (diffuse) Opportunity costs (cost of not directing resources to other problems) Symbolic or intangible benefits and costs How do we account for costs and benefits? 2.5 Program monitoring versus program assessment

Program – set of rules, routines and resources intended to carry out a policy Program monitoring or performance measurement – are outputs consistent with plans? This is a routine agency function Program assessment – linking program outcomes to intended goals or objectives Program evaluation – using a deliberate and systematic research design to learn about the costs and impact of programs

Program assessment

Complements program monitoring or measurement Used to be required under the Government Performance and Results Act (1993) Published as part of the Program Assessment Rating Tool (PART) under Clinton and Bush Not continued under the Obama administration Assessment is one tool for policy analysis: how is a current program working?

Program evaluation

Implementation – from the KRA document: “what you plan to do, how you plan to do it, and who it is you want to reach.” Direct costs Outputs Performance – “Have you achieved the results/outcomes you expected?” Direct impact on target population For a detailed example of how evaluation works see: KRA Corporation. 1997. “A Guide to Evaluating Crime Control of Programs in Public Housing. U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development.

2.6 What’s new?

Moneyball for Government – using metrics and predictive modeling to improve program performance. Who benefits from what intervention? “Top tier evidence”

Big data – using existing large-scale administrative data sources to understand program impacts. (What are the major Federal Statistical Agencies (FSAs)?)

Machine learning and AI – using algorithms to learn about program performance and guide program innovation

2.7 Concerns about evaluation

Diverts agency resources from services to other functions Has workload implications for street-level bureaucrats Too complicated, especially compared to program monitoring Risks of negative findings Monitoring in another form, so duplicates effort Research design in evaluation

2.8 After analysis: what happens?

  • end the program: Program termination confronts many obstacles (constituencies within and outside of government)

  • reform the program: reform is more likely - triggering problem definition, development of alternatives, and a predictable cycle of reform and re-reform

  • do nothing: this is very common (actors with a stake in program operation prefer to ignore evaluation)
